About Us

What is FanHaeng?

A modern, respectful, and obsessively detailed travel guide built for the global generation of fans traveling to Korea.

FanHaeng (팬행) combines the Korean word for "fan" (팬) with "haeng" (행), meaning journey or travel. It is a name built on a simple idea: fans deserve a travel resource that takes their passion seriously.

We are not a gossip site. We do not scalp tickets. We do not promote sasaeng behavior or invasive fan culture. FanHaeng exists to help you experience Korea — its concerts, its cafes, its filming locations, its neighborhoods, its culture — with respect, confidence, and the deepest possible preparation.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Real answers to the questions every K-pop and K-drama fan asks before traveling to Korea.

Start by aligning your trip dates with concert or comeback schedules — check official fan clubs and ticketing platforms like Interpark and Melon Ticket at least three months ahead. Book accommodation in Gangnam, Hongdae, or near COEX for the best access to venues, fan cafes, and entertainment districts. Learn a few basic Korean phrases, download Naver Map and KakaoTalk, and get a T-money transit card at Incheon Airport on arrival. FanHaeng's pillar guides walk you through every step from visa prep to post-concert etiquette.

Most Korean concerts sell tickets through platforms like Interpark, YES24, or Melon Ticket, and you will need a verified Korean phone number or a ticketing proxy service to purchase them. Membership in official fan clubs often grants priority ticketing windows, so join your group's Weverse or Daum Cafe community early. Arrive at the venue well ahead of the doors — Korean fans queue politely and early — and be sure to check the venue's bag and lightstick policies in advance. FanHaeng's Concert Travel guide covers ticketing timelines, venue maps, fan-zone logistics, and what to do if you cannot secure a ticket.

The best neighborhoods depend on your venue. For KSPO Dome and Olympic Park shows, stay near Jamsil or Songpa for a short subway ride. For Gocheok Sky Dome, Mokdong or Yeouido keeps you close. Hongdae is the all-around best base for nightlife, fan cafes, and general accessibility on Subway Line 2. Budget travelers love guesthouses in Mapo-gu, while luxury seekers book Gangnam hotels near COEX. FanHaeng's Seoul Neighborhoods guide rates every district by concert access, dining, and vibe.

Fan cafes in Seoul are temporary pop-up events organized by fan unions (fanbases) to celebrate an idol's birthday, comeback, or debut anniversary. They typically run for a few days at a rented cafe space, offering free or low-cost themed drinks, cupsleeves, photocards, and decorated photo zones. You usually need to purchase at least one drink from the hosting cafe to participate, and some events require reservations via Twitter or Instagram. FanHaeng's Fan Cafe guide tracks active events, maps their locations across Seoul, and explains etiquette so you never feel lost.

Birthday cafe schedules change monthly and are announced on Twitter/X and Instagram by individual fan unions, usually one to three weeks before the event. Hotspot neighborhoods include Hongdae, Gangnam Station, Sinchon, and areas around HYBE and SM Entertainment buildings. FanHaeng's Fan Cafe pillar page maintains a regularly updated list of confirmed events with dates, addresses, and transit directions. You can also search the Korean hashtag format — for example, searching the idol's name plus "birthday cafe" or "생일카페" — on social media for the latest drops.

Korea is filled with iconic filming spots. In Seoul, Bukchon Hanok Village and Gyeongbokgung Palace appear in dozens of historical dramas, while Namsan Tower and the Han River parks are modern drama staples. Outside the city, Nami Island is famous from "Winter Sonata," Jumunjin Beach from "Goblin," and the Gamcheon Culture Village in Busan from several recent series. FanHaeng's K-Drama Filming Locations guide catalogs over 50 verified locations with scene references, photos, transit info, and recommended visiting hours.

Attending live music shows such as Inkigayo (SBS), Music Bank (KBS), M Countdown (Mnet), Music Core (MBC), and The Show (SBS MTV) typically requires fan club membership and winning a lottery or pre-registration. Each show has its own application process — some use the Weverse or Daum Cafe platforms, while others require the broadcaster's own app. Recordings usually happen on weekdays with pre-dawn queuing, so plan for early mornings and bring essentials like a portable charger and snacks. FanHaeng's Music Show guide breaks down each show's application process, dress code, recording schedule, and what to expect inside the studio.

Hongdae is the heartbeat of fan culture — packed with fan cafes, idol merch shops, busking stages, and affordable eats open late. Gangnam and Apgujeong are where you will find entertainment company buildings (SM, JYP, HYBE) and upscale fan-oriented stores. Myeongdong is great for K-beauty shopping between fan activities, and Itaewon offers international dining for when you need a break. FanHaeng's Seoul Neighborhoods guide profiles every major district with fan-relevance scores, top picks, and walking routes.

Nami Island and Petite France are classic K-drama pilgrimage destinations reachable in under two hours by train or bus from Seoul. The Korean Folk Village in Yongin is a filming location for historical dramas and offers a full-day cultural experience. For K-pop fans, a trip to the MBC Daejanggeum Park drama set or the Hybe Insight museum makes a rewarding outing. Busan, reachable in 2.5 hours by KTX, offers Gamcheon Culture Village, Haeundae Beach, and its own concert scene. FanHaeng's Day Trips guide covers ten essential outings with transport instructions, time estimates, and cost breakdowns.

Korea is widely regarded as one of the safest countries in the world for solo travelers, including women. Public transit runs late, streets are well-lit, and violent crime rates are extremely low compared to most Western countries. That said, standard travel precautions still apply: stay aware in crowded concert queues, keep valuables secured in busy districts like Myeongdong, and save the address of your accommodation in Korean on your phone. FanHaeng's Safety and Etiquette guide covers solo travel tips, emergency numbers (112 for police, 119 for ambulance), cultural norms, and a fan-specific safety checklist.
