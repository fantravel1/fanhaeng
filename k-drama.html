Why K-Drama Location Travel Matters

There is a moment that every K-drama fan knows. You are watching a scene unfold — a confession on a rain-soaked bridge, a quiet walk through autumn leaves, a tearful goodbye at a palace gate — and something in you says: I want to be there. Not just see it. Be there. Stand on that exact spot. Feel the air. That impulse is the heart of drama location travel, and it is more valid than anyone who has not felt it could ever understand.

Korea's drama tourism industry has grown into a multi-billion-won economic force. Local governments now actively court production companies, knowing that a single memorable scene can turn an unknown village into an international destination overnight. Jumunjin Beach went from quiet coastal town to pilgrimage site after Goblin. Nami Island became a global landmark because of Winter Sonata. The Starfield Library in COEX barely opens its doors before fans are recreating scenes from a half-dozen different dramas.

What makes this form of travel so compelling is the way locations become cultural landmarks. A bench is no longer just a bench once two characters sat there and changed each other's lives. A stairway becomes sacred ground. A convenience store parking lot becomes the most romantic place in the world. Drama location travel is about seeing the ordinary through the lens of story — and that transforms how you experience an entire country.

The "scene recreation" phenomenon deserves its own mention. Fans visit specific locations to recreate exact moments from their favorite dramas — same angle, same pose, sometimes even the same outfit. Done well, it is joyful and deeply personal. It is not about clout or content. It is about closing the distance between a story you love and the real world where that story was born. This guide will help you do it with intention, respect, and the practical knowledge to make every visit count.

Top Seoul Filming Locations

Seoul is the undisputed capital of K-drama filming. Nearly every drama set in modern Korea has at least a handful of scenes shot somewhere in this city, and many dramas are filmed almost entirely within its boundaries. These are the locations that matter most for drama fans — the ones worth building a trip around.

Bukchon Hanok Village

Bukchon is the neighborhood that appears in more Korean dramas than possibly any other single location. Nestled between Gyeongbokgung Palace and Changdeokgung Palace, this cluster of hundreds of traditional hanok houses has served as the backdrop for countless sageuks (historical dramas) and modern romance series alike. The narrow alleyways climbing up and down the hillside create naturally cinematic compositions that directors cannot resist.

You have seen these streets in Personal Taste, Lovers in Paris, and dozens of other productions. The most photographed viewpoint is the famous Bukchon 8-kyeol (Alley 8), where a row of hanok rooftops frames a distant view of the city. Early morning visits (before 9 AM) are essential — this is a residential neighborhood, and by midday it swells with tour groups.

Namsan Tower / N Seoul Tower

If Bukchon is the king of historical drama locations, Namsan Tower is the undisputed queen of Korean romance. This iconic tower sitting atop Mount Namsan has appeared in so many love stories that it has essentially become a symbol of Korean romantic love itself. The love lock fence, the observation deck, the winding paths up the mountain — each has hosted confessions, first kisses, and heartbreaking farewells.

Dramas like Boys Over Flowers, My Love from the Star, and Crash Landing on You have all used Namsan Tower as a key romantic setting. The love lock fence is the single most recreated drama photo spot in Seoul. Visit at sunset for the most dramatic lighting, then stay for the tower's night illumination. The cable car ride up adds to the romantic atmosphere, though the walking path offers more photo opportunities.

Gyeongbokgung Palace

Gyeongbokgung is the grand palace of Korean historical dramas. Built in 1395 as the main royal palace of the Joseon dynasty, its sprawling courtyards, ornate throne halls, and stone-walled corridors have been the setting for power struggles, royal romances, and political intrigue across generations of sageuks. When you watch a historical drama and see a king holding court or a queen walking through moonlit gardens, there is a good chance you are looking at Gyeongbokgung or a set inspired by it.

Dramas like Jewel in the Palace (Dae Jang Geum), Moon Embracing the Sun, and Scarlet Heart Ryeo owe much of their visual grandeur to this palace. Many visitors rent hanbok (traditional Korean clothing) and explore in costume, which grants free admission and makes for stunning photos. The changing of the guard ceremony at the main gate, Gwanghwamun, happens daily and is itself a dramatic spectacle.

Ihwa Mural Village

Perched on the slopes of Naksan in the Hyehwa area, Ihwa Mural Village is a neighborhood transformed by art. What was once a declining hillside community became a canvas for murals, sculptures, and installations — and then became a favorite filming location for indie and slice-of-life dramas that wanted something more textured than the polished glass of Gangnam.

The colorful walls, steep staircases, and rooftop views have appeared in dramas like Rooftop Prince and various web dramas. The village has a raw, authentic energy that translates beautifully on screen and in photographs. Be mindful that this is a real residential area — some murals have actually been painted over by residents frustrated with tourist intrusion. Visit quietly, respect boundaries, and let the art come to you.

Banpo Bridge

Banpo Bridge stretching across the Han River is Seoul's most cinematic nighttime location. Its Moonlight Rainbow Fountain — the world's longest bridge fountain — shoots jets of water illuminated in shifting colors, creating a backdrop that drama directors have used for climactic confessions, contemplative walks, and dramatic reunions.

The bridge and surrounding Banpo Hangang Park appear in dramas like The Heirs, City Hunter, and many others. The riverside park below is where characters go to think, eat ramyeon from convenience stores, and have those quiet conversations that change everything. Visit during the fountain show (April through October, multiple shows nightly) for the full experience. The south bank park offers the best viewing angles.

Starfield Library at COEX

The Starfield Library inside COEX Mall in Gangnam has become one of the most instantly recognizable modern drama locations in Korea. Its two towering bookshelves — rising over 13 meters high and holding some 50,000 books — create a visual so striking that it has appeared in dramas, variety shows, music videos, and countless social media posts since its opening in 2017.

You have seen it in Extraordinary Attorney Woo, Touch Your Heart, and other modern dramas where characters meet, study, or have chance encounters in impossibly beautiful public spaces. The library is free and open to the public, located in the heart of COEX's underground shopping complex. For the cleanest photos, visit during weekday mornings. The enormous scale of the shelves makes even smartphone photos look cinematic.

Iconic Locations Outside Seoul

While Seoul dominates K-drama filming, some of the most legendary drama locations lie beyond the capital. These are the places worth a day trip or even an overnight stay — locations so deeply connected to specific dramas that visiting them feels like stepping directly into the story.

Nami Island (Winter Sonata)

Nami Island is where Korean drama tourism began. When Winter Sonata aired in 2002 and became a pan-Asian phenomenon, the half-moon-shaped island in the North Han River transformed from a quiet nature retreat into the most famous drama filming location in the world. The tree-lined pathways where Bae Yong-joon and Choi Ji-woo walked together became the most visited drama location in Korean history.

More than two decades later, Nami Island remains a pilgrimage site. The metasequoia lane, the ginkgo tree path, and the central garden area are all maintained with the drama legacy in mind. Each season transforms the island completely — cherry blossoms in spring, lush canopy in summer, golden ginkgo in autumn, and snow-covered paths in winter. The ferry ride from Gapyeong Wharf takes about 10 minutes, and the island is accessible by the ITX-Cheongchun train from Seoul's Yongsan or Cheongnyangni Station in about 60 to 90 minutes.

Petite France, Gapyeong

Located in the same Gapyeong region as Nami Island, Petite France is a French-themed cultural village that gained fame as a filming location for My Love from the Star and the variety show Running Man. The pastel-colored European-style buildings perched on a hillside create a surreal contrast with the Korean mountain landscape — exactly the kind of visually unexpected setting that drama directors love.

The village is compact and can be toured in about an hour. Combine it with a Nami Island visit for a full Gapyeong day trip. The best photos come from the upper terraces looking down over the colorful rooftops. Admission includes access to galleries, a marionette theater, and various exhibition spaces themed around Antoine de Saint-Exupery's The Little Prince.

Jumunjin Beach, Gangneung (Goblin)

The BTS stop (bus stop, that is) at Jumunjin Beach in Gangneung became one of the most visited drama locations in Korea after Goblin (Guardian: The Lonely and Great God) aired in 2016-2017. The scene where Kim Shin and Ji Eun-tak meet at the seaside bus stop is one of the most recreated drama moments in fan tourism history. The actual bus stop has been preserved and marked as a drama filming site, complete with informational signage.

Gangneung itself is a beautiful coastal city on Korea's east coast, accessible from Seoul in about two hours via the KTX high-speed train (a route that became dramatically more accessible after the 2018 PyeongChang Winter Olympics infrastructure improvements). The beach is gorgeous year-round, though autumn and winter — matching the drama's timeline — carry the strongest emotional resonance.

Gamcheon Culture Village, Busan

Built on the steep hillsides overlooking Busan's harbor, Gamcheon Culture Village is a kaleidoscope of pastel-painted houses, street art, and narrow staircase alleys that has earned it the nickname "the Machu Picchu of Busan." Originally a settlement for Korean War refugees, the village has been revitalized through art projects and has become a favorite filming location for dramas seeking colorful, character-rich backdrops.

The village has appeared in multiple dramas and films, and its unique terraced architecture offers photo opportunities around every corner. Small galleries, cafes, and shops are woven into the residential streets. Budget about two to three hours for a thorough visit. The viewing platforms at the top of the village offer sweeping panoramas of the colorful houses cascading down to the ocean.

Jeju Island Locations

Jeju Island, Korea's largest island located off the southern coast, is a drama filming powerhouse. Its volcanic landscapes, dramatic coastlines, tangerine orchards, and distinctive stone walls have provided settings for dozens of productions. The island's unique geography — unlike anything on the Korean mainland — gives dramas an otherworldly quality that directors use to signal emotional turning points, romantic escapes, or fresh beginnings.

Key drama filming spots on Jeju include Seopjikoji (a coastal promontory featured in All In and The Heirs), Hallim Park, Seongsan Ilchulbong (Sunrise Peak), and various beaches and cafes along the coastal road. Jeju is accessible by a one-hour flight from Seoul or Busan, and the island is best explored by rental car over two to three days. Many filming locations are spread across the island, making a car essential for efficient drama location hopping.

Best K-Drama Photo Spots in Seoul

Scene recreation is an art form. Done thoughtlessly, it is just a tourist snapshot. Done with intention, it becomes a genuine connection between you and a story that moved you. Here is how to approach K-drama photo spots in Seoul with skill and respect.

Where Exactly to Stand for Scene Recreation

The key to a great scene recreation photo is matching the camera angle, not just the location. Before you visit, screenshot the specific scene you want to recreate and study it closely. Note the angle (low, eye-level, high), the direction the character faces, and what is visible in the background. At the location, hold your screenshot up and move around until the background elements align. Often, the perfect spot is a few meters from where most people stand.

  • Namsan Tower love lock wall: Stand at the southwest corner of the main lock fence for the angle used in most drama close-ups. The tower is visible over your right shoulder.
  • Bukchon Alley 8: Position yourself at the midpoint of the downhill slope and shoot looking uphill toward the hanok rooftops. This matches the most common establishing shot.
  • Starfield Library: Stand at the center of the ground floor and look straight up between the two shelf walls. Or position yourself at the second-floor railing for the character perspective shot.
  • Banpo Bridge: The south bank of the Han River, about 100 meters east of the bridge's center, gives you the classic wide-angle fountain shot with the city skyline.

Time of Day Recommendations

Lighting makes or breaks a photo, and drama scenes are almost always filmed with careful lighting consideration. Match the scene's mood with the right time of day.

  • Golden hour (sunrise/sunset): Namsan Tower, Bukchon Hanok Village, any outdoor location. The warm light replicates the soft, romantic look of most drama cinematography.
  • Blue hour (just after sunset): Banpo Bridge, Cheonggyecheon Stream, any nighttime scene location. The sky transitions to deep blue, and city lights begin to glow.
  • Overcast midday: Actually excellent for Ihwa Mural Village and covered locations. Even, diffused light eliminates harsh shadows and brings out colors in murals and architecture.
  • Night: Essential for any Han River scene, Gangnam streetscapes, and Myeongdong. Use your phone's night mode liberally.

Seasonal Considerations

Korean dramas are deeply seasonal, and many iconic scenes derive their emotional power from the specific season in which they were filmed.

  • Cherry blossoms (late March to mid-April): Yeouido Hangang Park, Seokchon Lake near Lotte World, and Namsan pathways. The window is narrow — typically just seven to ten days of peak bloom.
  • Autumn foliage (mid-October to mid-November): Nami Island, Deoksugung Stone Wall Road, and virtually everywhere in Seoul. This is the most photographed season in Korea.
  • Snow (December to February): Namsan Tower, palace grounds, and Nami Island. Fresh snowfall in Seoul is relatively infrequent, so be flexible with your plans if snow scenes are a priority.
  • Summer greenery (June to August): Bukchon, palace gardens, and Han River parks. Lush and vibrant, though hot and humid. Early morning is essential.

Non-Cringe Photo Tips

Let's be honest: the line between heartfelt scene recreation and awkward tourist photo is thin. Here is how to stay on the right side.

  • Commit to the moment. If you are going to recreate a scene, own it fully. Half-hearted poses always look worse than going all-in.
  • Bring a friend or use a timer. Solo photos at drama spots work, but having someone take candid-style shots as you walk through the location produces more natural results.
  • Don't block pathways. Move quickly, take your shots, and step aside. Nothing ruins the magic faster than other visitors glaring at you.
  • Quality over quantity. One well-composed recreation photo is worth more than twenty blurry attempts. Slow down, frame it carefully, and nail it.

Respect at Residential Locations

Several popular K-drama filming locations — Bukchon Hanok Village, Ihwa Mural Village, and various neighborhood streets — are real places where people live. Keep your voice down. Do not peer into windows. Do not use tripods or drones in narrow alleys. Follow posted signage about photography restrictions. Remember: the residents did not sign up to live on a drama set. Your respect preserves these locations for future fans.

How to Plan a K-Drama Location Route

The biggest mistake drama fans make is treating each location as an isolated destination. Seoul is a massive city, and bouncing between locations on opposite sides of town will eat your day in subway transfers. The smart approach is to cluster locations by area and build routes that flow naturally.

Grouping Locations by Neighborhood

Seoul's drama locations cluster into natural groupings that align with the city's geography.

  • Northern Seoul (Jongno area): Bukchon Hanok Village, Gyeongbokgung Palace, Changdeokgung Palace, Insadong, Ihwa Mural Village. These are all within walking distance or one subway stop of each other.
  • Central Seoul (Myeongdong-Namsan): Namsan Tower, Myeongdong streets, Cheonggyecheon Stream, N Seoul Tower Cable Car. Connected by foot or short bus rides.
  • Southern Seoul (Gangnam-COEX): Starfield Library, Bongeunsa Temple, Gangnam streets, Apgujeong Rodeo Street. The Gangnam area is a drama world of its own.
  • Han River: Banpo Bridge, Yeouido Hangang Park, Ttukseom Hangang Park. Riverside locations work best as evening additions to a daytime itinerary.

Sample 1-Day Seoul Drama Route

This route covers the highest-impact locations in a single efficient day.

  1. 8:30 AM — Bukchon Hanok Village. Arrive early for empty streets and morning light. Photograph the key alleys and viewpoints. Allow 60 to 90 minutes.
  2. 10:00 AM — Gyeongbokgung Palace. Walk south from Bukchon. Rent hanbok near the entrance for free admission and photos. Watch the guard-changing ceremony if timing aligns. Allow 90 minutes to 2 hours.
  3. 12:30 PM — Lunch in Insadong. Traditional Korean restaurants and tea houses abound. Rest and refuel.
  4. 2:00 PM — Ihwa Mural Village. Subway from Anguk to Hyehwa (one stop on Line 4). Explore the murals and hillside views. Allow 60 minutes.
  5. 4:00 PM — Namsan Tower. Subway to Myeongdong, then cable car or walk up Namsan. Time your arrival for late afternoon light transitioning to sunset. Allow 2 hours including transit.
  6. 7:00 PM — Banpo Bridge. Subway from Namsan area to Express Bus Terminal Station. Walk to the Banpo Hangang Park for the evening fountain show. End the day riverside.

Sample 2-Day Seoul + Nami Island Route

For fans who want to include locations outside Seoul, this two-day itinerary adds the legendary Gapyeong area.

Day 1: Follow the 1-Day Seoul Drama Route above.

Day 2:

  1. 8:00 AM — Depart Seoul. Take the ITX-Cheongchun train from Yongsan or Cheongnyangni Station to Gapyeong (about 60 to 90 minutes).
  2. 10:00 AM — Nami Island. Ferry from Gapyeong Wharf. Spend the morning walking the tree-lined paths, recreating scenes, and photographing the island at your pace. Allow 2 to 3 hours.
  3. 1:00 PM — Petite France. Take the Gapyeong City Tour Bus or a taxi. Lunch at the village cafe and explore the galleries. Allow 1 to 2 hours.
  4. 3:30 PM — Return to Seoul. Train back to Seoul, arriving by late afternoon. Use the evening to visit Starfield Library at COEX or explore Gangnam.

Transport Between Locations

Seoul's public transit is world-class and will get you between drama locations efficiently.

  • Subway: The backbone of Seoul navigation. Most drama locations are within a 10-minute walk of a subway station. Lines 1, 3, and 5 cover the most drama-relevant areas.
  • Bus: Useful for hillside locations (Ihwa, Bukchon upper areas) where subway stations are at the bottom of steep climbs. Naver Maps shows real-time bus arrivals.
  • Taxi: Affordable by international standards. Use the Kakao T app for easy pickup. Taxis are essential for reaching locations like Nami Island ferry terminal from Gapyeong Station.
  • Walking: Many drama clusters are best explored on foot. Bukchon to Gyeongbokgung is a 15-minute walk. Myeongdong to Namsan Cable Car is 10 minutes. Build walking time into your route.

Seasonal Guide — When to Visit

Korea's four distinct seasons dramatically change the character of every filming location. The season you choose for your drama pilgrimage will shape not just your photos but the entire emotional texture of your trip.

Spring (March to May): Cherry Blossom Locations

Spring is when Korea transforms into the romantic backdrop of every first-love drama. Cherry blossoms (beotkkot) bloom across the country from late March through mid-April, peaking in Seoul around the first week of April. The bloom period is heartbreakingly short — typically just seven to ten days — so timing is everything.

  • Yeouido Cherry Blossom Road: The most famous cherry blossom spot in Seoul, and a filming location for multiple romance dramas. The tunnel of blossoms along the National Assembly road is breathtaking.
  • Seokchon Lake (near Lotte World): A quieter alternative with cherry trees ringing the entire lake. The Lotte Tower in the background adds a distinctly modern Seoul flavor.
  • Namsan pathways: The walking trails up Namsan are lined with cherry and forsythia. The combination of spring blossoms and Namsan Tower creates quintessential drama scenery.
  • Palace grounds: Both Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung are stunning during cherry blossom season. Hanbok plus cherry blossoms equals peak Korean aesthetic.

Summer (June to August): Beach and Waterfront Scenes

Korean summers are hot and humid, but they bring drama locations to life with lush greenery and vibrant energy. Summer is the best time for coastal and waterfront filming locations, where the heat is tempered by ocean breezes.

  • Jumunjin Beach: The Goblin bus stop location is at its most active in summer, with the beach itself offering a lively atmosphere.
  • Jeju Island: Summer is peak season for Jeju, with the island's hydrangeas in full bloom (June-July) adding purple and blue hues to the landscape.
  • Han River parks: Summer evenings along the Han River capture the energetic, social side of Seoul that dramas often depict. Convenience store ramyeon at Banpo Park hits differently at night.
  • Indoor locations: When the humidity becomes overwhelming, retreat to air-conditioned drama spots like Starfield Library. Summer is actually an ideal time for indoor locations with fewer tourists.

Autumn (September to November): The Most Photogenic Season

Autumn is universally regarded as the best season to visit Korea, and it is the season most frequently depicted in K-dramas. The foliage transformation — from green to gold, orange, and crimson — gives every location a warm, cinematic glow that no Instagram filter can replicate.

  • Nami Island: The golden ginkgo tree lane in October and November is the single most iconic autumn image in Korean drama tourism.
  • Deoksugung Stone Wall Road: This Seoul walking path beside the palace wall is a legendary drama location, and autumn leaves make it unforgettable.
  • Bukchon Hanok Village: The traditional rooftops framed by autumn foliage create a scene that looks like a painting.
  • Everywhere else: Seriously, every drama location in Korea improves in autumn. Plan for mid-October through mid-November for peak color.

Winter (December to February): Snow Scene Recreations

Winter is the season of emotional intensity in K-dramas — breakups, long-awaited reunions, tearful confessions in the snow. If your favorite drama scenes involve winter coats, white breath in cold air, and snowflakes, this is your season.

  • Nami Island: Snow-covered tree lanes directly recreate the most famous scenes from Winter Sonata. This is the pilgrimage at its purest.
  • Namsan Tower: The tower and surrounding paths dusted in snow are achingly romantic. Fewer tourists brave the cold, giving you cleaner photos.
  • Palace grounds: Fresh snowfall on the palace courtyards creates images that look straight out of a historical drama. Check weather forecasts and rush to the palaces after overnight snow.
  • Seoul streets: Winter street scenes — hot street food, breath in the cold air, holiday illuminations — capture the cozy, intimate side of Korean drama life.

Local Respect & Cultural Notes

Drama tourism has brought enormous economic benefits to Korea, but it has also created real friction in some communities. Being a thoughtful visitor is not just about manners — it is about ensuring these locations remain accessible and welcoming for future fans.

Many Filming Locations Are Real Businesses and Residences

This is the most important point in this entire guide. Bukchon Hanok Village, Ihwa Mural Village, and countless other filming locations are not sets — they are real places where real people live, work, and go about their daily lives. The convenience store from your favorite drama is someone's livelihood. The alley where the lead characters kissed is someone's walk home. Treat these places accordingly.

Don't Disrupt Locals

  • If a shop or restaurant was featured in a drama but has moved on to regular business, do not demand drama-related accommodations. Order something, be a good customer, and mention the drama only if the staff seems receptive.
  • Do not follow residents or take photos of people's homes without permission.
  • If a local asks you to move or stop photographing, comply immediately and graciously.

Photography Boundaries

  • Some Bukchon alleys have posted no-photography zones. Respect them absolutely.
  • Do not use tripods, selfie sticks, or ring lights in residential areas without explicit permission.
  • At palaces and cultural sites, follow posted photography rules. Some interiors prohibit flash or photography entirely.
  • Drone photography is heavily regulated in Korea, especially near government buildings and military zones. Do not fly drones at filming locations without proper permits.

Noise Levels at Quiet Locations

Korean neighborhoods are often remarkably quiet. A group of excited fans talking at normal volume can feel loud in a hillside village at 8 AM. Keep your voice low, especially in residential areas and at temples. If you are traveling with a group, designate meeting points rather than calling out to each other across narrow streets.

Warning: Residential Areas

In Bukchon Hanok Village and Ihwa Mural Village, residents have organized to restrict tourist access during certain hours. Some homeowners have posted signs asking visitors not to photograph their homes. Some murals in Ihwa have been deliberately painted over by frustrated residents. This is a direct response to disrespectful tourism. If you want these locations to survive as accessible drama spots, your behavior during your visit directly matters.

Drama Location Travel Essentials

Practical preparation makes the difference between a smooth drama pilgrimage and a frustrating day of getting lost and missing locations. Here is what you need.

T-money Card for Transport

The T-money card is a rechargeable transit card that works on all Seoul buses, subways, and even taxis. You can buy one at any convenience store (CU, GS25, 7-Eleven) for about 4,000 won and load it with credit as needed. Tap on and off at subway gates and bus readers. This single card eliminates the need to buy individual tickets at every station and saves you a small amount on each fare compared to single-use tokens. It also works on the ITX trains to Gapyeong and many intercity buses.

Naver Maps vs Google Maps in Korea

This is critical: Google Maps does not work reliably for navigation in Korea. Due to national security regulations, Google does not have access to detailed Korean map data. Walking directions are often inaccurate, transit routes may be wrong, and some locations simply will not appear. Use Naver Maps instead. It is available in English, provides accurate walking and transit directions, shows real-time bus arrivals, and includes interior maps of subway stations. Download the app before your trip and learn to search in both English and Korean (for locations that may not have English names in the database).

Pro Tip: Naver Maps

Naver Maps also has a "street view" equivalent that can help you identify exact filming locations before you arrive. Use it during your pre-trip planning to match your drama screenshots to real-world landmarks, so you know exactly where to go when you are on location.

Screenshot Your Drama Scenes Before Visiting

This might be the most practical advice in this entire guide. Before you leave your accommodation each morning, review the locations on your itinerary and save screenshots of the specific drama scenes you want to recreate or reference. Save them to a dedicated album on your phone. When you arrive at each location, pull up the screenshot and use it as a real-time reference for finding the exact filming spot and angle. WiFi is not always reliable at outdoor locations, so having screenshots saved locally is essential.

Comfortable Walking Shoes: Non-Negotiable

Drama location travel involves significantly more walking than typical sightseeing. Bukchon is hilly. Namsan is a mountain. Ihwa Village is a steep hillside. Gamcheon in Busan requires climbing hundreds of steps. Palace grounds are vast. You will walk 15,000 to 25,000 steps on a dedicated drama location day. Wear proper walking shoes — not fashion sneakers, not sandals, not the shoes that look good but hurt after an hour. Your feet will determine whether you enjoy the day or endure it.

Data and WiFi for Navigation

Reliable internet access is essential for real-time navigation between filming locations. Options include renting a portable WiFi device (available at Incheon Airport upon arrival), purchasing a Korean SIM card or eSIM, or relying on Korea's extensive free public WiFi network (though this is less reliable in residential neighborhoods and outdoor locations). A portable WiFi device or eSIM is strongly recommended — you will use it constantly for Naver Maps, translation apps, and looking up drama scene references on the go.
